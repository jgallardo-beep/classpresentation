Quick Recall

IT is the tool. IS is the whole system.

Analogy: IT is the guitar. An Information System is the whole band—people, instruments, rules, and coordination—working together to produce useful output.

Core Concept

What is an Information System?

An Information System is an organized combination of people, hardware, software, networks, data, and procedures that collects, processes, stores, and distributes information to support decisions and operations.

Simple formula: People + Technology + Process = Better Information and Decisions

Building Blocks

Essential elements of IS

People

Users, managers, customers, and IT personnel.

Hardware

Computers, mobile devices, scanners, and servers.

Software

Programs, applications, and operating systems.

Data

Facts stored and processed by the system.

Networks

Connections that allow communication and sharing.

Procedures

Rules for correct, secure, and consistent use.

Why IS Exists

Three main purposes

Support Operations

Handle transactions, record keeping, and daily activities.

Support Decisions

Provide reports, summaries, and analysis to managers.

Support Strategy

Help organizations innovate, compete, and grow.

School Examples

Information Systems around you

Enrollment System

Collects student data, processes requests, and creates class lists.

Grading System

Stores scores, computes grades, and generates reports.

Library System

Tracks books, borrowers, due dates, and inventory.

Historical Context

Evolution of
Information Systems

From paper ledgers to cloud platforms and artificial intelligence.

Era 1 · Pre-1960s

Manual Information Systems

Characteristics

  • Paper records and ledgers
  • Filing cabinets
  • Manual calculations
  • Typewritten documents

Limitations

  • Slow processing
  • High error rate
  • Difficult retrieval
  • No real-time reporting
Era 2 · 1960s–1970s

Mainframe and EDP Systems

What Changed

  • Large mainframe computers
  • Electronic Data Processing
  • Batch processing
  • Payroll, inventory, and accounting automation

Mainframes often filled an entire room and were affordable mainly to large corporations and government institutions.

Era 3 · 1980s–1990s

Personal Computer Revolution

Key Innovations

  • PCs on office desks
  • Spreadsheets and word processors
  • Desktop databases
  • Email communication

Impact

  • End-user computing
  • Higher individual productivity
  • Access for small businesses
  • Local digital storage
Era 4 · Late 1990s–2000s

Network and Internet Era

Connectivity

Computers and organizations connected globally.

E-Commerce

Websites became platforms for buying and selling.

Enterprise Systems

ERP connected multiple departments using shared data.

Communication

Email and instant messaging became standard.

Web Databases

Records could be accessed through browsers.

Early Mobile

Portable access began to expand.

Era 5 · 2010s–Present

Cloud, Mobile, and AI Era

Cloud

Systems available anywhere through the internet.

AI and ML

Systems recognize patterns and support decisions.

Mobile-First

Services designed for phones and tablets.

IoT

Connected devices continuously collect data.

Examples include Google Classroom, online banking, GCash, school portals, and ride-hailing platforms.

Classification

Types of
Information Systems

Systems may be classified by organizational hierarchy and by business function.

By Organizational Level

The IS hierarchy

TPS

Operational level. Detailed daily transactions.

MIS

Middle management. Routine summary reports.

DSS

Senior management. Analysis and what-if models.

ESS

Executive level. Strategic dashboards and trends.

Level 1 · Operational

Transaction Processing System (TPS)

What it does

Records and processes routine daily transactions.

  • Point-of-sale transactions
  • ATM withdrawals
  • Enrollment entries
  • Attendance encoding

Characteristics

  • High transaction volume
  • Repetitive and structured
  • Fast and accurate
  • Foundation of higher-level systems
TPS in Action

Everyday examples

Store POS

Scans products, records sales, updates inventory, and prints receipts.

ATM

Processes withdrawals, deposits, transfers, and balance inquiries.

School Attendance

Records whether learners are present, absent, or late.

Level 2 · Middle Management

Management Information System (MIS)

What it does

Transforms TPS data into routine reports used for monitoring and control.

  • Monthly sales reports
  • Grade summaries
  • Budget utilization reports
  • Attendance summaries

Key Features

  • Scheduled reports
  • Structured information
  • Predefined formats
  • Supports department managers
Level 3 · Senior Management

Decision Support System (DSS)

DSS helps managers make semi-structured or non-routine decisions using data analysis, models, forecasts, simulations, and what-if scenarios.

What-If Analysis

What happens if the budget is reduced by 10%?

Modeling

Simulates possible outcomes before taking action.

Interactive

Allows users to change assumptions and compare options.

Level 4 · Executives

Executive Support System (ESS)

Purpose

Provides summarized, visual, strategic information for long-term planning.

  • KPI dashboards
  • Revenue and profit trends
  • Market analysis
  • Competitor intelligence

Characteristics

  • Highly summarized information
  • Visual graphs and dashboards
  • Internal and external data
  • Strategic focus

Second Classification

IS by Business
Function

Systems can also be grouped according to the department or organizational function they support.

Major Functional Areas

IS serves every department

Finance IS

Budgets, cash flow, investments, and forecasts.

Marketing IS

Customers, campaigns, competitors, and sales.

HRIS

Hiring, attendance, payroll, and employee records.

Production IS

Inventory, scheduling, quality, and operations.

Accounting IS

Ledgers, taxes, statements, and compliance.

Academic IS

Grades, curriculum, classes, and schedules.

Key Takeaways

What we covered

  • IS combines people, technology, data, networks, and procedures.
  • IS evolved from manual systems to cloud, mobile, and AI platforms.
  • Hierarchy types are TPS, MIS, DSS, and ESS.
  • Functional systems support finance, marketing, HR, production, accounting, and academics.
  • All systems depend on accurate and reliable data.
